Chapter I Appearance and Reality

Is there any knowledge in the world so certain that no reasonable person could doubt it? This question, which at first glance seems unproblematic, is in fact one of the most difficult questions that can be posed. As soon as we become aware of the obstacles encountered in seeking a direct and reliable answer, we are already deeply immersed in philosophical inquiry—for philosophy is nothing other than an attempt to answer such fundamental questions. Yet unlike everyday life or even science, philosophy does not answer questions hastily or dogmatically; rather, it first examines precisely what makes these questions puzzling, recognizes the vagueness and confusion lurking within our ordinary ideas, and only then offers answers critically.

In daily life, we take many things to be true; yet upon closer inspection, they turn out to be so full of obvious contradictions that only careful reflection can tell us what we may genuinely believe. When investigating truth, we naturally begin with our present experience, and in some sense, knowledge undoubtedly arises from these experiences. But any statement about what direct experience actually reveals to us may very well be mistaken. I feel that I am now sitting on a chair, facing a table of a certain shape, and I see sheets of paper with writing on them. Turning my head, I see buildings outside the window, along with clouds and the sun. I believe the sun is about ninety-three million miles away from Earth; I believe it is a hot celestial body many times larger than Earth; I believe the sun rises every morning due to Earth’s rotation and will continue to do so in the future. I believe that if an ordinary person were to enter my room, he would see these chairs, tables, books, and papers just as I do; and I believe the table I see is the very same table my hand is pressing against. All this seems so obvious that it hardly merits mention—except perhaps when responding to someone who doubts whether I can know anything at all. Yet before we can be sure we have expressed these things in a completely truthful form, we have reason to doubt everything, and all of it requires much careful discussion.

To clarify our difficulties, let us focus attention on this table. It appears rectangular, brown, and glossy; to the touch, it feels smooth, cold, and hard; when I tap it, it produces a wooden sound. Anyone seeing, touching, or hearing the table would agree with this description, so it seems no difficult problem arises—at least until we try to be more precise, at which point our troubles begin. Although I believe the table is “really” uniformly colored, the parts reflecting light appear much brighter than the rest, and due to this reflection, certain areas seem white. I know that if I move my position, the reflective areas will change, and thus the distribution of apparent colors on the table’s surface will also change. Clearly, if several people look at the table simultaneously, no two will see exactly the same color distribution, since no two can occupy precisely the same viewpoint, and any change in viewpoint alters the way light reflects.

For most practical purposes, these differences are insignificant; but for a painter, they are extremely important. The painter must abandon the habitual assumption that objects possess the colors common sense deems they “really” have; instead, he must cultivate the habit of seeing objects as they appear. Here we encounter a distinction that constitutes one of philosophy’s greatest difficulties—the distinction between “appearance” and “reality,” between what things seem to be and what they really are. The painter wants to know what things seem like; the practical person and the philosopher want to know what they really are—and the philosopher’s desire is stronger than the practical person’s, and he is more troubled because he knows how difficult the question is to answer.

Returning to the table, based on what we have observed, it is clear that no single color stands out as the color of the table or of any particular part of it—different viewpoints yield different colors, and there is no reason to suppose that some of these colors are more truly the table’s color than others. Moreover, even from a fixed viewpoint, colors may appear different due to artificial lighting, or because the observer is color-blind or wearing blue-tinted glasses; and in darkness, there is no color at all, even though the table remains unchanged to touch or sound. Thus, color is not something inherently belonging to the table itself, but something dependent on the table, the observer, and the way light falls upon it. When we speak in everyday life of the table’s color, we merely mean the color it appears to have for a normal observer under ordinary lighting conditions from a typical viewpoint. Yet other colors revealed under different conditions have equal claim to being “real”; therefore, to avoid bias, we must deny that the table itself possesses any unique color.

The same applies to its texture. To the naked eye, one can see the grain of the wood, yet from another angle the table appears smooth and flat. If we view it through a microscope, we see rough hills and valleys and all sorts of differences invisible to the unaided eye. Which of these is the “real” table? Naturally, we tend to say that what the microscope reveals is more real—but viewed through an even higher-powered microscope, it would change again. If we cannot trust what we see with the naked eye, why should we trust what we see through a microscope? Thus, the senses from which we started turn out to be unreliable after all.

Nor is the situation any better regarding the table’s shape. We are accustomed to judging objects by their “real” shapes, and we do so so automatically that we think we actually see those real shapes. But in fact, if we wish to draw, we must learn that a given object appears differently shaped from different viewpoints. If our table is “really” rectangular, then from almost any viewpoint it will appear to have two acute angles and two obtuse angles. If opposite sides are parallel, they will seem to converge toward a point in the distance from the observer. If opposite sides are equal in length, the nearer side will appear longer. Usually, none of these facts are noticed when looking at a table, because experience has taught us to infer the “real” shape from its appearance—and it is this “real” shape that interests us as practical beings. But the “real” shape is not what we actually see; it is something inferred from what we see. Moreover, as we walk around the room, what we see constantly changes its shape; thus, once again, the senses seem not to give us truth about the table itself, but only about its appearances.

We encounter the same difficulty when we consider touch. Indeed, the table always gives us a sensation of hardness, and we also feel its resistance to pressure. But the sensations we obtain depend both on how much pressure we apply to the table and on which part of our body we use to press against it; thus, the various different sensations arising from differing pressures or from different parts of the body cannot be regarded as directly revealing any definite property of the table—they are at most signs of some property, a property which may be the cause of all these sensations, yet which clearly does not itself appear in any of them. The same reasoning evidently applies to the sound produced by tapping the table.

It is thus evident that the real table, if it exists at all, is not identical with the table we directly experience through sight, touch, or hearing. If a real table does exist, it is not something we directly know, but must instead be inferred from what we do directly know. Hence, two very difficult questions arise: (1) Is there really such a thing as a real table? (2) If so, what kind of object could it possibly be?

Several simple terms with clear meanings can help us consider these two questions. Let us call the things directly known in sensation “sense-data”: such as colors, sounds, smells, hardness, roughness, and so on. We shall call the experience of directly apprehending these things “sensation.” Thus, whenever we see a color, we have a sensation of that color, but the color itself is a sense-datum, not a sensation.

Color is something we directly apprehend, but the act of apprehension itself is sensation. This is quite obvious: if we are to know the table, we must necessarily do so through sense-data—brownness, rectangularity, smoothness, etc.—which we associate with the table; and for the reasons given above, we cannot say that the table is identical with the sense-data, nor that the sense-data are straightforwardly properties of the table. Thus, assuming there is such a thing as a real table, the question arises concerning the relation between sense-data and the real table.

If the real table exists, we shall call it a “physical object.” We must therefore investigate the relation between sense-data and physical objects. The totality of physical objects is called “matter.” Our two questions can thus be restated as follows: (1) Is there really such a thing as “matter”? (2) If so, what is its nature?

The first philosopher to formally argue that the immediate objects of our senses cannot exist independently of us was Bishop Berkeley (1685–1753). His *Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous, in Opposition to Sceptics and Atheists* sought to prove that matter, as such, does not exist at all, and that nothing exists in the world except minds and their ideas. Hylas had always believed in matter, but he was no match for Philonous, who relentlessly drove him into contradictions, making Philonous’s own denial of matter seem like plain common sense. The arguments Berkeley employed vary greatly in value: some are important and correct; others are confused or ambiguous. Nevertheless, Berkeley deserves credit for showing that the existence of matter can quite reasonably be denied, and for pointing out that if anything exists independently of us, it cannot be the immediate object of our sensations.

When we ask whether matter exists, we are dealing with two distinct questions, and it is important to distinguish them clearly. Ordinarily, we use “matter” to refer to something opposed to “mind,” something we suppose occupies space and is fundamentally unrelated to any kind of thought or consciousness. It is primarily in this sense that Berkeley denies matter; that is, he does not deny that the sense-data we normally take as signs of the table’s existence are signs of something existing independently of us, but he firmly maintains that this “something” cannot be “non-mental”—that is, it cannot be neither a mind nor an idea in some mind. He admits that when we leave the room or close our eyes, something must continue to exist, and that our having seen the table gives us good reason to believe that something continues to exist even when we are not looking at it. However, he holds that this “something” cannot be radically different in nature from what we see, nor can it be entirely independent of being perceived, although it must be independent of our particular act of perceiving. Thus, he concludes that the “real” table is an idea in the mind of God. Insofar as we can only infer it and never directly apprehend it, this idea possesses the necessary permanence and independence from us, yet it is not something wholly unknowable—as matter would be.

Other philosophers after Berkeley have held similar views, maintaining that although the table does not depend on my seeing it for its existence, it does depend on being perceived (or otherwise sensed) by some mind—though not necessarily God’s mind, but often by the unified mind of the universe as a whole. Like Berkeley, they hold this view chiefly because they consider it impossible for anything to be real—certainly, nothing that is known can be real—except minds and their thoughts and feelings. Their argument in support of this view can roughly be stated as follows: “Anything that can be thought of is an idea in the mind of the thinker; therefore, nothing except ideas in minds can be thought of; therefore, anything else is inconceivable, and whatever is inconceivable cannot exist.”

To my mind, this argument is fallacious; those who advance it do not usually state it so bluntly or explicitly. But whether valid or not, this argument has been widely presented in various forms, and many philosophers—perhaps most—hold the view that nothing is real except minds and their ideas. Such philosophers are called “idealists.” When they attempt to explain matter, they either, like Berkeley, say that matter is really just a bundle of ideas, or, like Leibniz (1646–1716), claim that everything appearing as matter is in fact more or less a collection of primitive minds.

Yet these philosophers, despite denying matter as something opposed to mind, still admit matter in another sense. Recall that we posed two questions: (1) Is there really a real table? (2) If so, what kind of object could it be? Both Berkeley and Leibniz acknowledge that there is a real table, but Berkeley says it consists of certain ideas in the mind of God, while Leibniz says it is a collection of souls. Thus, both answer our first question affirmatively; it is only in answering the second question that they diverge from common opinion. In fact, nearly all philosophers seem to agree that there is a real table. They almost all agree that, regardless of our sense-data—

—color, shape, smoothness, and so on—how much they depend on us! Yet their appearance is a sign of something that exists independently of us; and this something, though perhaps entirely different from our sense-data, may be regarded as the cause of those sense-data when we stand in an appropriate relation to the real table.

It is now evident that the point on which philosophers agree—that, whatever the nature of the table may be, there is nevertheless a real table—is extremely important; and before proceeding further to discuss questions concerning the nature of the table, it is worth considering what reasons there are for accepting this view. Therefore, in the next chapter, we shall examine the grounds for assuming that there is a real table.

Before we go any further, it will be well to consider what we have discovered so far. It seems that if we take any ordinary object which we suppose to be knowable through the senses, what the senses directly tell us is not the truth about the object as it exists independently of us, but only the truth about certain sense-data; and as far as we can see, these sense-data depend upon the relation between ourselves and the object. Thus, what we directly see and feel is merely “appearance”; and we believe it to be a sign of some underlying “reality.” But if this reality is not at all like what appears, then do we have any means of knowing whether there is any reality at all? And if there is, can we discover what it is like?

Such questions are profoundly perplexing; even the most extravagant hypotheses are hard for us to rule out as false. Thus, the familiar table, which has never previously stirred our thoughts, has now become a problem brimming with astonishing possibilities. All we know about it is that it is not what it seems. Beyond this modest conclusion, we are free to speculate as we please. Leibniz tells us it is a colony of souls; Berkeley tells us it is an idea in the mind of God; and rigorous science, almost as astonishingly, tells us it is a vast collection of rapidly moving electric charges.

Among these startling possibilities, doubt further suggests to us that perhaps there is no table at all. Philosophy, even if it answers fewer questions than we might hope, at least has the right to pose questions that heighten our curiosity about the world and to reveal the strangeness and wonder lurking beneath the surface of the most ordinary things in daily life.

Chapter Two: The Existence of Matter

In this chapter, we must ask ourselves whether there is such a thing as matter at all, in any sense of the word. Is there a table possessing certain intrinsic properties that continues to exist even when I am not looking at it? Or is the table merely a product of my imagination, a table dreamed within a grand dream? This question is extremely important. For if we cannot be certain of the independent existence of objects, then neither can we be certain of the independent existence of other people’s bodies, and consequently, we cannot be certain of the existence of other minds either; for apart from the evidence derived from observing their bodies, we have no other grounds for believing they possess minds. Thus, if we cannot affirm the independent existence of objects, we would find ourselves utterly alone in a desert—perhaps indeed the entire external world is nothing but a dream, and only we ourselves truly exist. Yet this is an exceedingly unpleasant possibility; although it cannot be strictly proven false, there is also absolutely no reason to suppose it is true. In this chapter, we must understand why this is so.

Before we begin investigating this doubtful issue, let us first identify some point, however modest, that is already relatively certain as our starting point. Although we may doubt the physical existence of the table, we do not doubt the existence of the sense-data that lead us to believe there is a table; we do not doubt that when we look, we see certain colors and shapes; and subsequently, we can experience a sensation of hardness. All these mental phenomena we do not doubt. Indeed, anything might be doubted, but at the very least, some of our immediate experiences seem absolutely certain.

Descartes (1596–1650), the founder of modern philosophy, devised a method—the systematic method of doubt—that remains useful even today. He resolved never to believe anything to be true unless he perceived it with utmost clarity and distinctness. Anything he considered possibly doubtful, he doubted, until he reached something indubitable. By employing this method, he gradually came to believe that the only existence he could fully affirm was his own. He imagined an evil demon presenting him continuously with deceptive illusions through his senses; although such a demon seemed implausible, Descartes acknowledged it was still possible, and thus it was legitimate to doubt everything perceived through the senses.

However, doubting his own existence was impossible, for if he did not exist, no demon could deceive him. If he doubted, then he necessarily existed; if he had any experience whatsoever, he necessarily existed. Thus, his own existence was absolutely certain to him; as he famously declared, “I think, therefore I am” (cogito, ergo sum). On this foundation of indubitable certainty, he set out anew to reconstruct the world of knowledge that his doubts had demolished. By inventing the method of doubt and by demonstrating that subjective things are the most certain, Descartes made a great contribution to philosophy, rendering himself still valuable to all who study philosophy today.

Yet caution is required when employing Descartes’ argument. The statement “I think, therefore I am” actually asserts somewhat more than what is strictly certain. It seems we can all be quite sure that we today are the same selves as we were yesterday, and in some sense this is undoubtedly true. But the real self is just as difficult to attain as the real table, and it apparently lacks the absolute, compelling certainty belonging to particular experiences. When I look at my table and see a certain brown color, what I can immediately affirm with complete certainty is not “I see brown,” but rather “brown is seen.” Of course, this involves something (or someone) that sees the brown; yet it does not inherently include that more or less persistent entity we call “I.” As far as immediate certainty goes, it is quite possible that the something which sees brown is entirely momentary and not identical with the something having different experiences in the next moment.

Thus, what possesses primitive certainty is our particular thoughts and feelings. This applies not only to normal perception but equally to dreams and hallucinations. When we dream or see a ghost, we genuinely have—and believe we have—the sensation of seeing a ghost; yet for various reasons, we may maintain that no physical object corresponds to these sensations. Therefore, our awareness of our own experiences possesses an exact and unquestionable certainty that admits of no limitation or exception. Here, at any rate, we have a solid foundation from which to begin our pursuit of knowledge.

What we must consider is this question: Even if we affirm the existence of our sense-data, do we have any reason to believe these are signs of the existence of something we call a physical object? When we enumerate all the sense-data we naturally associate with a table, have we thereby exhausted everything there is to say about the table? Or is there something else beyond sense-data that continues to exist even when we leave the room? Common sense answers without hesitation that there is. Something that can be bought and sold, pushed around, and covered with a cloth cannot possibly be merely a collection of sense-data. If we completely cover the table with a cloth, we receive no sense-data from the table; thus, if the table were truly nothing but sense-data, it would cease to exist, and the cloth would miraculously float unsupported in the place where the table had been. This view is obviously absurd, yet to be a philosopher one must train oneself not to fear absurdity.

The principal reason we feel there must be a physical object beyond sense-data is our desire that different people share the same object. It would be absurdly ridiculous to insist that when ten people sit around a dining table, they do not see the same tablecloth, the same knives, forks, spoons, and glasses. Yet sense-data are personal to each individual, and what is directly presented to one person’s visual field is not identical to what is directly presented to another’s; everyone views things from slightly different perspectives and thus sees them slightly differently. Therefore, if there really exists a common, neutral object that, in some sense, can be known by many different people, then there must be something beyond and above the individual sense-data experienced by different persons. But what reason do we have to believe such a common, neutral object exists?

The first answer that naturally occurs to us is this: although different people may see the table with slight variations, what they see is still broadly similar, and the various differences among their perceptions conform systematically to laws governing distance and reflection of light; hence it is easy to conclude that a persistent object underlies all these differing sense-data. I purchased this table from the previous tenant of this room; I could not buy his sense-data, which vanished when he left; but I could—and indeed did—buy the expectation of obtaining sense-data reasonably similar to his. Thus, the fact is that different people have similar sense-data, and one person at different times, provided they occupy appropriate positions, will also have similar sense-data. This enables us to hypothesize

There must be, beyond and above the data of sense, a persistent common object which serves as the basis or cause of the sense-data experienced by different people at different times.  

All the foregoing considerations assume that there are other people besides ourselves; to that extent alone, they commit the fallacy of begging the question. Other people appear before us only through certain sense-data, such as their appearance or their voices; but if I have no reason to believe that there exist objects independent of my sense-data, then I have no reason to believe in the existence of other people except as parts of my dream. Thus, when we wish to demonstrate that there must be objects existing independently of our own sense-data, we cannot appeal to the testimony of others, since that testimony itself consists of sense-data and could reveal others’ experiences only if our own sense-data serve as signs of things existing independently of us. Therefore, if possible, we must find within our own private experience certain features sufficient to prove—or at least to render probable—that besides ourselves and our private experiences, other things also exist in the world.  

In one sense, it must be admitted that we can never prove the existence of things outside ourselves and beyond our own experience. The hypothesis that the world consists solely of myself, my thoughts, feelings, and sensations, and that everything else is mere speculation, involves no logical contradiction. In dreams, an extremely complex and intricate world may seem to exist, yet upon waking we discover it was illusory. That is to say, we find that the sense-data in dreams do not correspond to the physical objects we naturally infer from our sense-data. (Indeed, once we assume the existence of a physical world, physical causes can be found for dream sense-data: for example, the sound of a door slamming might cause us to dream of a naval battle. But in such a case, although the sense-data have a physical cause, there is no corresponding object—such as an actual naval battle—that matches the sense-data.) The hypothesis that our entire life is a dream, in which we ourselves create all the objects appearing before us, is logically not impossible. Yet, although this hypothesis is not logically impossible, there is no reason to suppose it true; indeed, as an explanation of the facts of our lives, it is far less simple than the common-sense assumption that there really exist objects independent of us, whose action upon us causes our sensations.  

If we assume that physical objects truly exist, the problem naturally becomes simpler; this is obvious. Suppose a cat appears in one corner of a room at one moment and in another corner the next moment; we naturally assume that it has moved from one part of the room to another through a continuous series of intermediate positions. But if the cat were merely a collection of sense-data, it could not have passed through places where I did not see it; thus, we would have to assume that it simply ceased to exist when I was not looking at it and suddenly reappeared in a new place. If the cat exists whether I see it or not, then, based on our own experience, we can understand how it gradually grows hungry between meals; but if it does not exist when I am not looking at it, then it seems absurd that its hunger should increase just as rapidly during non-existence as during existence. Moreover, if the cat consisted solely of sense-data, it could not be hungry at all, since no hunger except my own could ever become a sense-datum for me. Thus, the behavior of those sense-data that present themselves to me as a cat—though it seems perfectly natural to interpret them as expressions of hunger—becomes extremely puzzling if regarded merely as movements and changes of patches of color; just as a triangle cannot play football, so a patch of color cannot feel hunger.  

However, the difficulty presented by the example of a cat is far less serious than that presented by human beings. When people speak—that is, when we hear certain sounds associated with ideas, and simultaneously see certain lip movements and facial expressions—it is hard to imagine that what we hear is not an expression of thought; for we know that if we ourselves produced the same sounds, they would indeed express thoughts. Of course, similar situations occur in dreams, where we may be mistaken about the existence of others. But dreams are always more or less suggested by our waking life, and if we assume that a physical world truly exists, dreams can to some extent be explained scientifically. Thus, every principle of simplicity leads us to accept the natural view that, besides ourselves and our sense-data, there really exist objects whose existence does not depend on our awareness of them.  

Of course, we do not originally come to believe in an independent external world through argument. We find that we already hold this belief as soon as we begin to reflect; it is what is called an instinctive belief. In vision, the sense-data themselves are instinctively believed to be independent objects, though reasoning shows that objects cannot be identical with sense-data; yet we never doubt this belief. This discovery is in no way contradicted in cases of taste, smell, and hearing, and only slightly so in touch. Nevertheless, we still believe that there really are objects corresponding to our sense-data, and our instinctive belief is not thereby weakened. Since this belief raises no difficulties and instead simplifies and systematizes the account of our experience, there is no reason not to accept it. Thus, despite the doubts raised by dreams about the external world, we may still admit that the external world truly exists and that its existence does not depend on our continual awareness of it.  

The argument enabling us to reach this conclusion is undoubtedly not as strong as we might wish, yet it is typical of many philosophical arguments and therefore deserves brief consideration regarding its generality and validity. We find that all knowledge must be built upon our instinctive beliefs; if these are denied, knowledge becomes impossible. However, some of our instinctive beliefs are much stronger than others, and many of them, through habit and association, become entangled with other beliefs that are not in fact instinctive but are mistakenly regarded as part of our instinctive beliefs.  

Philosophy should clarify the hierarchy of our instinctive beliefs, beginning with those we hold most firmly, and should, as far as possible, isolate each belief from irrelevant accretions. It should be carefully noted that our instinctive beliefs, in their final form, must not contradict one another but should constitute a harmonious system. An instinctive belief should never be rejected unless it conflicts with others; thus, if they can be shown to cohere harmoniously, the whole system is worthy of acceptance.  

Certainly, all our beliefs—or any one of them—may be mistaken, so at least a slight degree of doubt should attach to every belief. But unless we base our rejection on some other belief, we have no reason to reject any belief.

a kind of belief. Thus, by organizing our instinctive beliefs and their consequences, examining which of them (if necessary) are susceptible to revision and which can be abandoned, and then accepting what we instinctively believe as our sole material, we can on this basis render our knowledge systematic and coherent—though the possibility of error remains, this possibility is diminished by the interrelations among its parts and by the critical scrutiny previously undertaken before acceptance.

This task, at the very least, is one that philosophy can accomplish. Most philosophers, rightly or wrongly, believe that philosophy can achieve far more than this—that it can provide us with knowledge about the universe as a whole and about the nature of the most fundamental “reality,” knowledge unattainable by any other means. Whether or not this is true, the more modest task we have described must be fulfilled by philosophy, and for those who have once begun to doubt the certainty of common sense, philosophy must surely suffice to justify the arduous and demanding labor involved in philosophical inquiry.

Chapter Three: Properties of Matter

In the previous chapter, although we could find no sufficient grounds for proof, we nevertheless agreed unanimously that it is reasonable to suppose that our sense-data—such as those which we associate with my table—are actually signs of something existing independently of us and our perceptions. That is to say, beyond and above the colors, hardness, sounds, and so on, which constitute for me the appearance of the table, I assume the existence of something else, of which these colors, hardness, sounds, etc., are merely phenomena. If I close my eyes, the color ceases to exist; if I move my arm away so that it no longer touches the table, the sensation of hardness ceases to exist; if I stop tapping the table with my finger, the sound also ceases to exist. Yet I do not believe that the table itself ceases to exist when all these sensations cease. On the contrary, I believe it is precisely because the table continues to exist that all these sense-data reappear when I open my eyes again, place my arm back, and start tapping the table once more. In this chapter, the question we must consider is: what is the nature of this real table, which persists independently of my perception?

To this question, physical science provides an answer. Admittedly, this answer is somewhat incomplete and partly hypothetical, yet in practice it remains noteworthy. Physical science, more or less unconsciously, tends toward the view that all natural phenomena should be reduced to motions. Light, heat, and sound all arise from waves, which travel from the object emitting them to the person who sees the light, feels the heat, or hears the sound. What undergoes these wave motions is either ether or “gross matter”; in any case, it is what philosophers call matter. The only properties science attributes to matter are occupying a position in space and the capacity to move according to laws of motion. Science does not deny that matter might possess other properties; but if it does, such properties are of no use to scientists and cannot help explain various phenomena.

It is sometimes said that “light is a form of wave motion,” but this is a misunderstanding; for the light we directly see—the light we know immediately through our senses—is not a form of wave motion at all, but something entirely different—a thing everyone who is not blind knows well, though we cannot convey our knowledge of light to a blind person. Waves, by contrast, can be very well described to a blind person, since the blind can acquire knowledge of space through touch; and after a sea voyage, they could experience waves almost as vividly as we do. But the waves understood by a blind person are not what we mean by light. The light we refer to is something a blind person can never understand, and we can never describe it to them.

This thing—which all of us who are not blind know, yet which, according to science, does not actually exist in the external world—is something produced in the eyes, nerves, and brain of the person who sees light by the action of certain waves. When we say light is waves, we really mean that waves are the physical cause producing in us the sensation of light. However, the actual light experienced by someone who can see—and which a blind person cannot experience—is not regarded by science as forming any part of the world that exists independently of us and our senses. A similar view applies to other sensations as well.

Not only are colors, sounds, and so on absent from the scientific material world, but even the space we encounter through sight and touch is likewise absent. For science, what matters is that the so-called material objects occupy space; yet the space they occupy cannot be exactly the same space we see or feel. First, the space we see is not the same as the space we touch; only through experience acquired from early childhood do we learn how to touch what we see, or how to look at what we feel is touching us. Scientific space, however, is neutral—intermediate between touch and sight—and therefore cannot be either tactile space or visual space.

Moreover, different people perceive the same object as having different shapes depending on their viewpoints. For example, a small round coin, though we always judge it to be circular, may appear elliptical unless we view it head-on. When we judge it to be circular, we are asserting that it possesses a real shape—one that is independent of its apparent shape and intrinsic to the object itself, regardless of how it appears. It is this real shape, relevant to science, that occupies a real space distinct from the space any individual perceives superficially. Real space is public, whereas the space perceived superficially belongs to the individual perceiver. In the personal spaces of different individuals, the same object seems to have different shapes; thus, the real space—in which objects possess their real shapes—must necessarily differ from personal spaces. Hence, although scientific space is related to the space we see and feel, it is not identical to it; and the nature of this relationship requires further examination.

For now, let us provisionally accept that physical objects, though not entirely resembling our sense-data, can nonetheless be regarded as the causes producing our sensations. These objects occupy scientific space, which we may call “physical” space. It must be noted that if our sensations are caused by objects, there must necessarily exist a physical space containing these objects, along with our sense organs, nerves, and brains. When we come into contact with an object, we receive a tactile sensation; that is, at that moment, the part of our body occupies a position in physical space very close to the position occupied by the object. In physical space, if no opaque body lies between the object and our eyes, we (roughly speaking) see the object. Similarly, we can hear, smell, or taste an object only when we are sufficiently close to it, when it touches our tongue, or when it occupies an appropriate position relative to our body in physical space. Unless we regard both a given object and ourselves as situated within a single physical space, we cannot state which different sensations we would obtain from that object under varying conditions; for it is primarily the relative positions of the object and our body that determine what sensations we receive from the object.

Now, our sense-data all reside within our personal space—either within visual space, tactile space, or those vaguer spaces provided by our other senses. If, as science and common sense assume, there exists a public, all-encompassing physical space in which all physical objects reside, then the relative positions of objects in physical space must correspond, at least approximately, to the relative positions of sense-data in our personal spaces. We can readily assume this to be the actual case. For instance, if we see one house on the road as nearer than another, our other senses support this judgment of relative nearness; for example, if we walk down the road, we reach the apparently nearer house first. Others will also agree that the house appearing closer to us is indeed nearer; military maps similarly produce

The same feeling; thus, everything indicates that the spatial relation between the two houses corresponds to the spatial relation among the sense-data we perceive when looking at the houses. Hence, we may assume that there indeed exists a physical space, in which the spatial relations among physical objects correspond to the spatial relations among the respective sense-data in our private space. This physical space is precisely what geometry investigates and what physics and astronomy presuppose.

Assuming that there really is a physical space, and assuming further that it corresponds to private space in this way, what can we know about it? All we can know is precisely what is required to preserve this correspondence. That is to say, we know nothing whatsoever about what it is like in itself, but we can know the arrangement of physical objects resulting from their spatial relations. For example, we can know that during an eclipse, the Earth, Moon, and Sun lie on a straight line, even though we do not know what a physical straight line actually is—just as we do know what a straight line looks like in our visual space. Thus, regarding distance relations in physical space, we know far more about the relations than about distances themselves; we can know that one distance is greater than another, or that it proceeds along the same straight line as another, yet we cannot have direct acquaintance with physical distances, since we only know distances—or colors, sounds, or other sense-data—within our private space. Everything we can know about physical space is analogous to what a person blind from birth could learn about visual space through others. But just as there are aspects of visual space that a person blind from birth can never know, so too there are aspects of physical space that remain unknowable to us. We can ascertain what characteristics relations must possess in order to correspond to sense-data; however, we cannot ascertain the intrinsic nature of the relata that constitute these relations.

Regarding time, our sense of temporal duration or the passage of time is evidently far less reliable than clocks. Time seems to pass slowly when we are anxious or in pain, quickly when we are happy, and almost as if it does not exist at all when we are asleep. Therefore, insofar as time consists of durations, it is necessary—as with space—to distinguish between public and private time. However, insofar as time involves an order of succession, no such distinction is needed; as far as we can tell, the temporal order events appear to have is the same as the temporal order they actually possess. In any case, we have no reason to suppose these two orders differ. The same generally holds true for space: if a group of people marches along a road, their formation will look different from different viewpoints, yet their sequential order remains the same from every viewpoint. Thus, we take this order to be valid in physical space as well, and we assume correspondence between shapes and physical space only insofar as maintaining this order requires it.

When we say that the apparent temporal order of events is the same as their actual temporal order, we must guard against a possible misunderstanding here. It must never be supposed that the different states of distinct objects share the same temporal order as the sense-data constituting our perceptions of those objects. Consider thunder and lightning as physical phenomena: they are simultaneous—that is, the flash and the disturbance of the air occur simultaneously at the location where the disturbance originates, namely, where the lightning occurs. However, the sense-datum we call "hearing thunder" does not occur until the air disturbance has traveled far enough to reach our location. Similarly, sunlight takes approximately eight minutes to reach us; thus, when we see the Sun, we see it as it was eight minutes ago. As far as the evidence provided by our sense-data about the material Sun is concerned, it is evidence about the material Sun as it existed eight minutes ago. If the material Sun had ceased to exist during those eight minutes, this would have no bearing whatsoever on the sense-datum we refer to as "seeing the Sun." This provides us with another illustration demonstrating that sense-data must be distinguished from physical objects.

Concerning space, what we see exhibits a correspondence roughly similar to that between sense-data and their physical counterparts. If one object appears blue and another red, we have reason to believe there is some corresponding difference between the two physical objects; if both appear blue, we may infer a corresponding similarity. Yet we cannot hope to gain direct acquaintance with the intrinsic properties that cause objects to appear blue or red. Science tells us that this property is a certain kind of wave motion, which sounds familiar because we recall waves in our visually perceived space. But in fact, these waves exist in physical space, about which we have no direct acquaintance. Hence, real waves lack the familiarity we imagine them to possess. An analogous explanation applies equally well to other sense-data. Thus, we find that although objects possess various knowable properties due to their relations corresponding to relations among sense-data, the intrinsic nature of objects themselves remains unknowable to us, no matter how thoroughly our senses operate. The question remains: is there any other method by which we might discover the intrinsic nature of physical objects?

At any rate, regarding visual sense-data, we may initially adopt the most natural (though ultimately not the most secure) hypothesis: although objects cannot be exactly like sense-data for the reasons already considered, they are nevertheless somewhat similar. According to this view, objects genuinely possess (for instance) color, and we might occasionally happen to see the actual color an object possesses. At any given instant, the color an object appears to have is generally extremely similar—though not identical—from many different viewpoints; thus, we may suppose that the "real" color is some intermediate shade lying between the various hues produced by differing perspectives.

This theory may perhaps be impossible to definitively refute, yet it can certainly be said to lack foundation. First, it is evident that the color we see depends solely on the nature of the light waves entering our eyes; consequently, it is constrained by how light reflects off the object toward the eye and by the intervening medium. Unless the air between us and the object is perfectly clear, the color will change; moreover, strong reflections can entirely alter the perceived color. Therefore, the color we see is merely the result produced when light reaches the eye, and not simply a property of the object from which the light emanates. Thus, whenever certain light waves reach our eyes, we will necessarily see some color, regardless of whether the object emitting those waves possesses color at all. Consequently, we have absolutely no need—and no legitimate grounds—to assume that physical objects possess color. Entirely analogous arguments apply to other sense-data as well.

It remains to ask whether there exists any general philosophical argument enabling us to assert that if matter is real, it must necessarily possess such-and-such a nature. As mentioned earlier, many philosophers—perhaps most—hold that whatever is real must necessarily be in some

In a sense, it is mental; or, at any rate, whatever we can know must necessarily be mental in some sense. Philosophers who hold such views are called “idealists.” Idealists tell us that whatever appears to be material is in fact something mental—that is, either (as Leibniz claimed) composed of primitive minds of some sort, or (as Berkeley proposed) ideas within minds; these, according to our ordinary way of speaking, are what “perceive” matter. Thus, although idealists do not deny that our sense-data are signs of something existing independently of our individual sensations, they do deny the existence of matter as something intrinsically different from mind. In the next chapter, we shall briefly examine the various reasons idealists have advanced in support of their theory—reasons which, in my view, are all mistaken.
